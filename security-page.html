Security

This document describes the security programme HelveX AG operates around the HelveX AI builder, the Hosted Services that run Customer-built applications, the AI generation pipeline, and the surrounding platform. It is the canonical reference for procurement, security, and engineering teams evaluating HelveX. It complements the Privacy Policy, the Data Processing Addendum, and the Trust Center.

EffectiveMay 2026 Last updatedMay 2026 Version1.0 Governing lawSwitzerland
§ 01

Security programme — framework and posture

HelveX operates a security programme structured around five operating principles. Defence in depth: protective controls are layered such that no single failure leaves the platform exposed. Least privilege: each identity — person, service, integration — receives the minimum access required to perform its function, and that minimum is reviewed periodically. Encrypt everything: data is encrypted in transit and at rest by default, and the keys protecting that data are managed in dedicated key-management infrastructure. Replayable audit: every meaningful action is logged in tamper-evident form with the actor, the time, the action, the target, and the outcome, so that an investigator can reconstruct what happened after the fact. Shared responsibility: the line between what HelveX secures and what the Customer secures is drawn explicitly and consistently across this document.

The control set is drawn from the recognised industry frameworks listed below. HelveX maps its internal controls against each framework's criteria, evaluates gaps on a defined cadence, and remediates findings on a risk-weighted basis.

  • SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria — Security, Availability, and Confidentiality categories
  • ISO/IEC 27001:2022 — Information Security Management System
  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF 2.0) — Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
  • OWASP Application Security Verification Standard (ASVS) — Level 2 minimum, Level 3 for security-critical components
  • Cloud Security Alliance Cloud Controls Matrix (CSA CCM) — operational reference for cloud-native components

HelveX designs and operates its security programme around controls drawn from the frameworks above. HelveX does not, in this document, claim certification against SOC 2 or ISO/IEC 27001. HelveX works toward independent attestation against these frameworks on an ongoing basis. Documentation describing HelveX's current control mapping, gap-assessment outputs, and the planned attestation timeline is available under mutual non-disclosure agreement via trust@helvex.ai.

The programme is owned by HelveX's Chief Information Security Officer, who reports to the CEO and to the Board's risk subcommittee on a quarterly cadence. The programme is reviewed in full annually; the review encompasses control adequacy, residual-risk acceptance decisions, the prior year's incident record, the prior year's audit findings, the threat-landscape outlook, and the plan for the year ahead. The programme materially adapts to AI-specific risks discussed in §7 and §14.

§ 02

Encryption

HelveX encrypts personal data and Customer Content in transit using TLS 1.3 with strong cipher suites and forward secrecy. TLS 1.2 is permitted on legacy ingress paths during defined deprecation windows and is excluded from defaults. Internal service-to-service traffic between HelveX components runs over mutually-authenticated TLS or equivalent transport. HSTS is set on all public origins. Certificate issuance is automated, certificates are short-lived, and certificate authority authorisation (CAA) records are enforced at the DNS layer.

At rest, Customer Content, AI Output, telemetry, and operational data are encrypted with AES-256-GCM. Database storage uses transparent data encryption (TDE) at the storage layer; object storage uses server-side encryption with HelveX-managed keys; backup snapshots are encrypted before they leave primary storage; and ephemeral disks attached to compute instances are encrypted at the hypervisor layer.

Key management is HSM-backed. HelveX uses the FIPS 140-2 or FIPS 140-3 validated key-management services of its primary cloud providers (AWS KMS / GCP Cloud KMS) with HSM-backed master keys. Master keys are rotated annually; data-encryption keys are rotated more frequently and on every material event (suspected compromise, personnel separation in a key-custodian role, audit-driven rotation). Key access is gated by role-based access controls; production-key access requires multi-party approval and is logged in tamper-evident form for after-the-fact review.

Enterprise Customers can opt into per-tenant key separation, in which the Customer's data is encrypted with key material that is logically (and on the highest enterprise tier, cryptographically) separated from other Customers' keys. Bring-Your-Own-Key arrangements with Customer-controlled cloud KMS instances are available on enterprise terms.

§ 03

Access control

Access to HelveX systems and Customer data is governed by a role-based access control (RBAC) model with least-privilege defaults. Roles are scoped to the minimum capabilities required for the function; privileged roles (production access, administrative roles, billing access, audit-log read access, key-custodian roles) are sparse and individually justified.

Authentication for HelveX personnel is enforced via single sign-on against the corporate identity provider, with mandatory phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication (hardware security keys for engineering and operations personnel). Long-lived secrets are not issued to personnel; access tokens for production systems are short-lived and bound to the underlying identity. Just-in-time elevation is required for production-data access: personnel request elevation with a documented justification, the request is reviewed by a separate approver, and the elevation expires automatically.

Authentication for Customer accounts supports password (with strong-password policy and breached-password detection), enterprise SSO over SAML 2.0 and OpenID Connect (OIDC), and multi-factor authentication via TOTP authenticator apps and WebAuthn/FIDO2 hardware keys. HelveX strongly recommends MFA for all Customer accounts and requires MFA for administrative roles within enterprise tenants. Brute-force, credential-stuffing, and impossible-travel detections are applied on the authentication surface.

Access reviews are conducted on a quarterly basis: each privileged role is recertified by its owner; orphaned access is removed; access that no longer matches the assignee's current responsibilities is recalibrated. Personnel separations (resignations, terminations) trigger automated de-provisioning on the same business day; the de-provisioning closes SSO sessions, revokes outstanding tokens, removes the assignee from privileged groups, and revokes hardware-key bindings.

§ 04

Network security

HelveX operates a zero-trust network architecture. Internal trust is not granted by network location; every service-to-service request is authenticated, authorised against an explicit policy, and logged. Internal services run inside private networks with no public ingress; public-facing endpoints are limited to the Customer-accessible API, the Builder UI, the documentation surfaces, and a handful of well-defined webhooks.

The production environment is segmented across three planes. The control plane runs the platform's orchestration logic, identity, billing, and AI-generation pipeline. The data plane runs storage and execution for Customer Content. The customer plane runs the Generated Applications that Customers have deployed. The three planes are isolated from each other by network-level boundaries; control-plane access to data-plane storage is mediated by audited APIs; data-plane access to customer-plane infrastructure is bounded by per-tenant policy.

Public endpoints sit behind a Web Application Firewall with rule sets tuned to the OWASP Top Ten, complemented by HelveX-authored rules tuned to attack patterns specific to AI applications (prompt-injection probes, generation-pipeline abuse, sandbox-escape probes). DDoS mitigation is provided at the edge via the cloud providers' native services and a third-party CDN, with the option to route ingress through an enterprise DDoS scrubbing partner on enterprise terms.

On enterprise terms, HelveX offers private connectivity options including AWS PrivateLink and GCP Private Service Connect, so that Customers can route their interactions with the Services over private network paths that do not traverse the public Internet.

§ 05

Infrastructure security

HelveX's production infrastructure runs on Amazon Web Services and Google Cloud Platform as its primary cloud providers, with regional pinning available to Customers and Microsoft Azure available for enterprise opt-in. The primary regions for processing of Customer Content are EU-Central (Frankfurt) and CH-Central (Zurich), with additional regions available on a Customer-specified basis.

Compute is built from hardened base images derived from CIS-Benchmark-aligned baselines, with HelveX-specific hardening layered on top. Images are scanned for vulnerabilities at build time and continuously revalidated; vulnerable images are pulled and replaced rather than patched in place. The infrastructure is immutable: changes are made by deploying new images, not by mutating running instances; the on-instance toolchain required for ad-hoc administration is absent by design.

Configuration is managed as code. Infrastructure-as-code (Terraform, Pulumi) and configuration-as-code (Helm charts, Kustomize overlays) are reviewed by peer engineers via the same pull-request workflow that governs application code. Configuration drift is detected by continuous reconciliation; out-of-band changes to production raise alerts and are reverted unless reauthorised through the standard workflow.

Patching is automated. Operating-system and container-image updates are applied through the immutable-redeploy mechanism on a defined cadence: critical patches within 24 hours of provider release, high-severity patches within 7 days, others within the routine release cycle. The mean time from patch availability to production rollout is tracked as an internal SLO.

§ 06

Application security and secure SDLC

HelveX engineers ship code through a secure software-development lifecycle anchored in a small number of mandatory practices.

Peer review. Every code change merges through a pull-request workflow that requires at least one approval from a qualified reviewer; security-sensitive areas (authentication, authorisation, key management, generation pipeline, sandbox, billing) require approval from a member of the security engineering team or a designated owner.

Static analysis (SAST). Every pull request is scanned by a suite of static analysers tuned to the languages and frameworks in use. Findings above a defined threshold block merge; below-threshold findings are tracked and burned down.

Dependency scanning (SCA). Software composition analysis runs on every build. Known-vulnerable dependencies above the configured severity threshold block the build until upgraded; dependencies without a fix are mitigated through compensating controls and tracked as exception items with explicit owners and review dates.

Dynamic analysis (DAST). Staging environments are scanned with dynamic analysers against the public Builder surface and the Customer API surface; findings flow into the same triage queue as static findings.

Secret scanning. Pre-commit and pre-push hooks plus repository-wide scanning detect committed secrets; any positive finding triggers immediate revocation of the affected credential and rotation across all systems that hold it.

Threat modelling. Architecture changes that introduce new trust boundaries, new data flows, or new categories of risk require a written threat model reviewed by security engineering before implementation begins.

Supply-chain integrity. HelveX produces a software bill of materials (SBOM) for each release; build provenance is signed; container images are signed and verified on deployment; third-party dependencies are pinned to specific versions and updated through the review pipeline rather than dynamically resolved at build time.

§ 07

AI-specific security

An AI builder concentrates a particular set of risks that a general-purpose SaaS platform does not. HelveX addresses each of them explicitly.

7.1 Prompt injection defences

HelveX treats every untrusted input — Customer prompts, content retrieved from a Customer's connected sources, content from third-party APIs reached during agentic execution, and content read from a Generated Application during execution — as a potentially adversarial instruction. Inputs are sanitised; system-instruction segments are isolated from user-content segments with structural and tokenisation boundaries; tool-call outputs are returned to the model as data, not as instructions; and context windows for one Project are isolated from those of any other Project. The output is itself classified for injection-relevant signals before it is acted on; suspected injections are flagged for human review or aborted.

7.2 Output safety filtering

Before AI Output is surfaced to the Customer's project workspace or stored to the Generated Application, it is passed through classifiers tuned to the categories of harm described in the Acceptable Use Policy: self-harm content, child sexual abuse material, sexual content involving minors, content that materially advances weapons-of-mass-destruction capability, targeted harassment, code identifiable as malware or exploit primitives, and others. The classifiers operate at multiple stages of the pipeline and are reviewed and updated regularly.

7.3 Code execution sandboxing

Generated Application previews — and any other code execution HelveX performs in the course of building, testing, or previewing a Customer's project — run inside isolated sandboxed environments with no privileged host access. Each sandbox has strict CPU, memory, disk, and wall-clock resource limits; an ephemeral filesystem that is wiped at the end of the session; restricted network egress to an allow-listed set of destinations; and no access to other Customers' sandboxes, the HelveX control plane, or the host's metadata service. The sandbox boundary is enforced by kernel-level isolation primitives plus userland confinement; egress is mediated by a per-sandbox network policy.

7.4 Model-supply-chain integrity

Foundation model artefacts used by HelveX are verified at load: artefact identifiers are pinned to specific versions; downloaded artefacts are checked against published hashes; provider trust is rooted in signed releases where the provider supports signing. Deprecation notices for upstream model versions are tracked and surfaced to Customers in advance of the deprecation date; HelveX maintains overlap windows during which Customers can migrate.

7.5 Rate limiting and abuse detection

The Builder applies per-account and per-endpoint rate limits to bound the volume of generation traffic a single account can produce. Anomaly detection runs on generation patterns to identify behaviour consistent with mass-scale jailbreak attempts, content laundering across many small requests, automated scraping of model behaviour, or generation patterns aimed at producing prohibited content categories at scale. Accounts that trip anomaly thresholds are subject to escalating responses up to and including suspension; the response ladder is documented in the AUP.

7.6 Training-data hygiene for HelveX-developed models

Where HelveX develops its own models, training data is curated for provenance, licence compliance, and presence of opt-out signals. HelveX-operated crawlers honour robots.txt opt-out directives addressed to HelveX-developed crawlers; published opt-out registries are honoured; and material identified post-training as having been collected in violation of policy is removed and the affected model is retrained or unlearned on a risk-weighted timeline. HelveX does not train HelveX-developed base models on Customer Content (Builder Inputs or AI Output).

§ 08

Hosted application isolation

HelveX hosts Generated Applications on a tenancy model designed to keep Customer environments isolated from each other.

On the shared infrastructure tier, Customer applications share the underlying physical and virtual infrastructure but are isolated at the logical layer: each Customer's compute runs in its own namespace with its own service identity, its own per-application network policy, its own storage scope, and its own secrets scope. Cross-tenant access is denied by default and is rejected by the policy layer even when the underlying network permits it.

On the dedicated infrastructure tier (available on enterprise terms), the Customer's applications run on dedicated compute and storage with no co-tenancy at the host level. Network paths from the public Internet to the Customer's dedicated infrastructure are also dedicated; observability data is captured into Customer-scoped storage; backups are taken into Customer-scoped buckets encrypted with Customer-scoped keys.

Per-Generated-Application resource limits are enforced (CPU, memory, request rate, storage quotas, egress quotas) so that a single Customer's application cannot exhaust shared capacity to the detriment of other Customers. Secrets isolation is structural: environment variables, OAuth grants, API keys, and database credentials provisioned to one Generated Application are not retrievable from another Generated Application or from another Customer's tooling.

§ 09

Logging, monitoring, and audit trails

HelveX logs every administratively significant action across the platform with the actor, the timestamp, the action, the target, and the outcome. The logged surface includes: authentication events (login, MFA challenge, password change, key rotation); generation events (prompt submission, model selection, generation outcome, classifier outcomes, token usage); deployment events (deploys, rollbacks, scaling actions, environment-variable changes); code change events (commits to project repositories where HelveX is the source of truth, file modifications, edits to project configuration); and administrative actions (invitations, role changes, billing changes, sub-processor configuration changes, audit-log access itself).

Logs are written to tamper-evident storage separated from operational systems: log entries are sequenced, integrity is verified continuously, and writes are append-only. Logs are encrypted at rest, access to logs is gated by privileged roles, and access to logs is itself logged.

A centralised SIEM ingests logs from the application surface, the cloud-provider control planes, the WAF, the authentication system, the generation pipeline, and the sandboxes. The SIEM applies detection rules, baseline anomaly detection, and threat-intelligence enrichment. Alerts are routed through an on-call rotation with 24/7 coverage; the rotation is staffed by trained security engineers backed by senior on-call escalation.

Internal audit reviews occur on a quarterly cadence: control owners attest to operating effectiveness, sample tests are performed by the security function, findings are tracked through remediation, and material results are summarised to the executive sponsor and the Board's risk subcommittee.

On enterprise terms, Customers can export their workspace audit trail and the runtime logs of their Generated Applications to their own SIEM via standard cloud-provider tooling (S3 with KMS keys, Cloud Storage with CMEK, or via a streaming integration). The export schema is documented in HelveX's Customer documentation.

§ 10

Vulnerability management

HelveX runs a layered vulnerability-management programme.

Internal scanning. Infrastructure is scanned for known vulnerabilities at least weekly across cloud-provider configuration, container images, and host-level state. Application code is scanned on every build (SAST), on dependencies (SCA), and against staging deployments (DAST). Web-application scanning probes the public surface for the OWASP Top Ten and HelveX-specific patterns.

Third-party penetration testing. Independent specialists conduct penetration testing at least annually, with additional engagements on material architecture changes (new trust boundaries, major refactors, new deployment models). Scope covers the Builder UI, the Customer API, the generation pipeline, the sandboxes, the deployment infrastructure, and the authentication surface. Penetration-test summaries are available to authorised Customer representatives under MNDA via trust@helvex.ai; full reports are reserved to HelveX security and the testers.

Remediation SLAs. Findings are triaged by severity. The remediation targets are as follows:

  • Critical — 24 hours from confirmed finding
  • High — 7 calendar days
  • Medium — 30 calendar days
  • Low — within the next routine quarterly remediation cycle

Exceptions to the SLA are recorded with an owner, a compensating control, and a review date; the exception list is reviewed at each internal audit cycle.

Coordinated disclosure programme. HelveX welcomes good-faith vulnerability research; researchers should report findings to security@helvex.ai. HelveX confirms receipt within five business days, triages the report, and works with the researcher on a coordinated disclosure timeline (default 90 days from confirmation). Researchers who comply with the programme's scope and conduct rules — no destruction of data, no denial-of-service against production, no access to other Customers' data, no public disclosure prior to coordinated release — are afforded legal safe harbour by HelveX.

§ 11

Personnel security

Personnel with access to Customer Content, AI Output, or production systems are subject to controls proportionate to their access.

Pre-employment background checks are conducted where lawful and proportionate. The check covers identity verification, right-to-work verification, prior-employment confirmation, and (for roles with privileged access) a criminal-record check and a reference review.

Every member of personnel signs a written confidentiality agreement as a condition of employment, with surviving obligations after the employment ends. The agreement is supplemented by the corporate acceptable-use policy and the information-security policy, both of which are acknowledged on onboarding and re-acknowledged annually.

Security training is delivered on onboarding and refreshed annually. Engineers receive role-specific training on secure coding, threat modelling, and AI-specific risks. Operations personnel receive role-specific training on incident response, on-call hygiene, and forensic preservation. All personnel receive training on phishing-resistance, social engineering, data classification, and incident reporting.

Separations trigger an integrated workflow that closes SSO sessions, revokes hardware-key bindings, removes the separating individual from privileged groups, retrieves issued devices, archives the individual's accounts in a non-recoverable state, and re-routes any responsibilities to a successor owner. Privileged-role separations additionally trigger a key-rotation review.

§ 12

Incident response

HelveX operates a documented incident-response programme. The programme covers detection, triage, containment, eradication, recovery, post-incident review, and external notification.

Detection sources include the SIEM (rule-based and anomaly-based detections), endpoint and runtime monitoring, internal employee reporting (any HelveX employee who suspects a security event is required to report it through the established channel), Customer reports (Customers who suspect a security event affecting their data should write to security@helvex.ai), external researchers (via the coordinated disclosure programme), and third-party intelligence sources.

The programme uses a severity classification matrix indexed on confidentiality impact, integrity impact, availability impact, regulatory exposure, and reputational exposure. Each severity tier maps to a defined response posture, a defined notification posture, and a defined post-mortem expectation.

Escalation paths are documented. The on-call security engineer is the first responder; an incident commander is appointed for incidents above the threshold; subject-matter experts are pulled in by domain; the executive sponsor (CISO) is notified for incidents above the threshold; the CEO and the Board's risk subcommittee are notified for incidents at the top of the matrix or for those with material legal or regulatory implications.

Customer notification. Where an incident constitutes a confirmed Personal Data Breach affecting Customer data within the meaning of Article 4(12) GDPR, HelveX notifies the affected Customer without undue delay and in any event within seventy-two (72) hours of confirmation. The notification includes the nature of the breach, the categories and approximate volumes of data affected, the likely consequences, the measures taken or proposed, and the contact point for further information, in line with Article 33(3) GDPR.

We commit to notifying affected Customers of a confirmed Personal Data Breach within seventy-two (72) hours of confirmation. Notification flows to the account-billing contact and to any additional security contacts the Customer has nominated. The Customer remains responsible for any onward notification to supervisory authorities and to its own Data Subjects, with HelveX providing reasonable assistance under the DPA.

Forensics and root cause. Every confirmed incident is followed by a formal post-mortem that identifies the root cause, the contributing factors, the timeline, the customer impact, and the remediation actions. Post-mortems are blameless and focus on systemic improvement. Material post-mortems are summarised to Customers via the account-billing contact and posted to /trust-center; aggregate annual transparency reporting summarises incident counts, response times, and remediation outcomes.

§ 13

Business continuity and disaster recovery

HelveX operates a business-continuity programme designed to maintain platform availability across the events catalogued in the corporate risk register and to recover from events that nevertheless materialise.

RPO and RTO targets. For the platform control plane (account, identity, generation pipeline orchestration, billing), HelveX's internal recovery-point objective is RPO ≤ 1 hour, and the internal recovery-time objective is RTO ≤ 4 hours. These targets are operational design points that drive backup cadence, replication topology, and failover testing; they are not contractual SLA commitments and the prevailing contractual SLA is the SLA published in the Order Form. Customer data planes have RPO and RTO targets adjusted to the tier on which the Customer's workload runs.

Backup strategy. Customer data is backed up continuously via storage-engine replication and via point-in-time recovery (PITR) snapshots taken on an automated schedule. Backups are stored in encrypted, geographically-replicated storage in at least one region distinct from the primary region. Backup restoration is tested on a defined cadence (sampled monthly; full restoration tested quarterly).

Multi-region failover. The control plane runs across multiple regions in active-passive (or active-active where the workload supports it) configurations. Failover to the secondary region is exercised at least once per year through a planned drill in which the secondary region serves production load for a defined window. Regional pinning of Customer data is honoured during failover: failover targets are selected within the Customer's regional scope where the Customer has specified one.

DR exercises. HelveX runs quarterly DR tabletop exercises that walk through a representative incident scenario with the response team. HelveX runs annual full-failover technical exercises in which the platform fails over to the secondary region, runs there for a defined window, and fails back. Findings from each exercise flow into the remediation backlog.

BCP review. The business-continuity plan is reviewed annually and after any material change to the platform architecture, the supplier landscape, or the threat landscape. The review is signed off by the CISO and reported to the Board's risk subcommittee.

§ 14

AI governance and responsible use

HelveX governs the AI components of its platform with practices that complement the technical controls in §7.

Model documentation. HelveX maintains model cards for HelveX-developed models that describe intended uses, out-of-scope uses, evaluation methodology, evaluation results in aggregate, known limitations, and the responsible-use guidance applicable to each model. Model cards are made available to Customers through the Builder UI and in HelveX's documentation.

Evaluation methodology. Models are evaluated before release against a battery of capability and safety evaluations, including evaluations targeted at the harm categories enumerated in the AUP. Evaluation methodology is disclosed in summary form; raw evaluation results are reserved to HelveX research and the relevant external reviewers.

EU AI Act preparedness. Where a Customer's intended use of the Services falls within scope of the EU AI Act (for example, where the Customer builds a Generated Application that performs a high-risk function), HelveX provides guidance on the transparency obligations that attach to AI-generated content, on the risk-categorisation considerations the Customer must assess, and on the documentation the Customer should prepare. HelveX does not, by providing such guidance, assume the Customer's role as the deployer of the Customer's own Generated Application.

Red-teaming and pre-release safety evaluation. Material model releases and material capability-enhancing changes to the platform are subject to red-team review prior to release. Red-teamers attempt prompt injection, content-policy circumvention, data-exfiltration, sandbox escape, and similar adversarial paths. Findings flow into remediation before release.

Responsible-disclosure of AI-specific issues. Researchers who identify AI-specific risks (model jailbreaks, prompt-injection patterns affecting agentic behaviour, output classifications) are invited to disclose through the same coordinated programme as conventional security issues; the safe-harbour terms apply.

§ 15

Customer responsibilities — shared responsibility model

The HelveX security programme is a shared model. The line is drawn explicitly here so that there is no doubt about who owns what.

HelveX is responsible for:

  • the security of the platform infrastructure (compute, storage, network, key management)
  • the integrity of HelveX-controlled foundation-model integration paths
  • the availability and isolation properties of the Hosted Services
  • internal access controls covering HelveX personnel
  • security patching of the Builder and the generation pipeline
  • monitoring and incident response on HelveX systems
  • the security characteristics of the sandboxes in which Generated Application previews execute
  • the integrity of the AI generation pipeline against prompt-injection and similar adversarial inputs

The Customer is responsible for:

  • the security of the Customer's account credentials and the enrolment of multi-factor authentication
  • the contents of Builder Inputs the Customer submits, including the appropriateness of any personal data the Customer chooses to include
  • the review, testing, and validation of AI Output before it is deployed into a production Generated Application or otherwise relied upon
  • the security of the Customer's Generated Application — the Customer's own dependency choices, authentication and authorisation logic the Customer chooses to implement, input validation and output encoding the Customer's application performs, secrets management within the Customer's application, and the operational practices the Customer applies to the Generated Application
  • the legal and operational relationship with the End Users of the Customer's Generated Applications, including the data the Customer chooses to collect from End Users and the safeguards the Customer applies to that data
  • the configuration of third-party integrations the Customer connects to the Builder or to the Generated Application (third-party API keys, OAuth scopes, webhook endpoints, identity providers)
  • compliance with any sector-specific regulation that applies to the Customer's industry or to the Customer's End Users (HIPAA, PCI DSS, financial regulation, education-privacy regimes, etc.)

The shared model is mandatory and the boundary above is the boundary. A security incident caused exclusively by the failure of a Customer-side control — a leaked Customer API key, an unsafe credential the Customer hardcoded into a prompt, an unreviewed AI Output the Customer deployed straight to production, a misconfigured identity provider the Customer integrated — is not a HelveX security incident. HelveX will nevertheless assist a Customer in good faith with investigation and remediation under the standard support channels.

§ 16

Reporting security issues

Security issues and vulnerability reports should be sent to security@helvex.ai. The mailbox is monitored every business day. PGP-encrypted reports are supported; the current public key is available on request from the same address.

HelveX confirms receipt within five business days, communicates a preliminary triage outcome within ten business days, and works with the reporter on a coordinated disclosure timeline of up to ninety (90) days from confirmation. Where the underlying issue requires a longer remediation window — for example, because a fix depends on upstream coordination or on a planned model retrain — HelveX communicates the revised timeline and the reasons.

Programme scope and conduct. Researchers are asked to limit testing to their own accounts, to refrain from accessing data belonging to other Customers, to refrain from disrupting production for other Customers, to refrain from denial-of-service tests against production, to refrain from social-engineering HelveX personnel or HelveX vendors, and to refrain from physical or supply-chain attacks. Research that respects these limits enjoys legal safe harbour from HelveX in respect of any Computer Misuse, Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, or equivalent statutory provision over which HelveX has standing to refrain from pursuit.

What HelveX expects in a report. A clear description of the issue, reproduction steps, the environment used, the impact assessment, and the reporter's preferred contact channel and disclosure preferences. PII or other sensitive material captured incidentally should be deleted by the reporter at HelveX's confirmation; HelveX does not require copies for its investigation.

§ 17

Frameworks and reports

HelveX maintains control-mapping documents that align HelveX's internal control set with each of the recognised industry frameworks named in §1. The mapping is reviewed annually and updated more frequently when the underlying framework or the HelveX control set changes materially.

The following artefacts are available to authorised Customer representatives and prospective Customers under mutual non-disclosure agreement via trust@helvex.ai:

  • HelveX's current control-mapping documents against SOC 2, ISO/IEC 27001, NIST CSF, and OWASP ASVS
  • The latest internal gap-assessment outputs against each framework
  • The planned timeline for independent attestation against each framework
  • Summaries of the most recent third-party penetration tests, with identifying detail redacted
  • The latest business-continuity test report
  • The latest privacy-impact and transfer-impact assessments
  • HelveX's incident-response plan and on-call playbooks (in summary form)
  • The current Sub-processor list (per Annex III of the DPA)

HelveX does not, in this document, claim certification against SOC 2 or ISO/IEC 27001. HelveX claims that its security programme is designed around controls drawn from these frameworks and that HelveX works toward independent attestation against them on an ongoing basis. Customers requiring formal attestation reports — for example, to satisfy their own vendor-management or regulator-facing obligations — can request HelveX's current control mapping and the planned attestation timeline under MNDA via trust@helvex.ai.

For laws (rather than certifications) HelveX is bound by, the binding statements are made in the Privacy Policy and the Data Processing Addendum: GDPR, Swiss FADP / nFADP, UK GDPR, CCPA / CPRA, and the Swiss Code of Obligations apply as a matter of law and HelveX complies with them as such.
