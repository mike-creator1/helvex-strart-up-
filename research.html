Principles

What we believe about AI.

Four ideas shape every research decision we make. They are not slogans — they are the lens we use when we choose what to work on, how to evaluate it, and what we are willing to ship.

Principle 01

Capability with character.

A model that is more capable should be more honest, more careful, and more useful — not just bigger. We measure character at the same level of seriousness as raw capability, and we will not ship a smarter model that is harder to live with.

Principle 02

Honesty over fluency.

A confident wrong answer is more harmful than a careful uncertain one. We invest in retrieval grounding, citation traceability, and calibrated uncertainty so that every claim a model makes can be checked, and so that it knows when it should not make one.

Principle 03

Useful work, not benchmarks.

Most public benchmarks reward a narrow definition of "smart." We evaluate on tasks that mirror what our customers actually do — multi-tool agent runs, long-context analysis, real documents — and publish where the public scores do and do not match real-world quality.

Principle 04

Auditable by default.

A model people can trust is a model whose work people can read. Every long agent run leaves a replayable trace. Every cited claim links back to the passage. Trust is built by making the inside of the system legible, not by promising it from the outside.

How we work

From experiment to product.

Our research lifecycle is short, repeatable, and on-record. Five stages, every project, no exceptions. The point is not the diagram — it's the discipline.

  1. Stage 01

    Hypothesis

    Every project starts with one written sentence: the claim we are trying to test, the population it should hold for, and the precise way we will know if we are wrong. The sentence lives in the project root and gets updated as the work progresses.

    Output: a hypothesis doc · 1 page · reviewed weekly

  2. Stage 02

    Experiment

    We design the smallest experiment that can falsify the hypothesis. Pre-registered eval set, frozen baseline, single variable changed. If a result is going to be reported, the experiment that produced it must be reproducible by another team member from the spec alone.

    Output: a runnable repo · pre-registered eval · weekly results log

  3. Stage 03

    Build

    If the experiment supports the hypothesis, the next step is a real implementation behind a feature flag. Engineering and research sit in the same room from this point on. The goal is to get the change in front of internal users so the failure modes show up early.

    Output: a flagged build · internal canary · failure mode log

  4. Stage 04

    Measure

    Before anything ships, we measure the change against the eval that was registered in stage two — and against a separate held-out set chosen by a colleague who was not on the project. We do not move forward on improvements that show up only on the eval we designed for ourselves.

    Output: a written eval report · holdout numbers · safety review

  5. Stage 05

    Publish

    Whether the project shipped or didn't, we write up what happened. Successful work becomes a paper or an engineering post; work that failed becomes a note. Either way, the result lives on this page so anyone can read what we tried and why.

    Output: a public paper, post, or note · added to Publications

Areas of focus

Where we spend the budget.

Six concentrations across the team. The list isn't exhaustive — it's where we believe progress on the next two years of AI most depends on careful, well-instrumented research.

01

Reasoning

How models allocate thought, when they should slow down, and what makes a chain of reasoning trustworthy enough to act on.

02

Long-horizon agents

Memory hierarchies, plan critique, deterministic replay — the engineering of agents that hold their head over hours of work.

03

Evaluation

Measuring what matters. Benchmarks that map to actual outcomes, and the honest limits of what current evaluations can claim.

04

Safety & alignment

Refusals, red-teaming, failure mode taxonomies, and the engineering of guardrails that don't degrade the model into unhelpfulness.

05

Multimodal grounding

Reading documents, charts, diagrams, scans, and audio with the same reliability as well-formatted text. The hard half of "multimodal."

06

Retrieval & citation

Hybrid search, permission-aware retrieval, and how to make every claim traceable to the source it came from.

Future vision

Where we are taking the work.

Three horizons we are building toward. The 12-month items are funded, staffed, and on the roadmap. The 24-month items are research we believe we can land but have not committed to. The long-term items are the questions that will define this decade of AI — we expect to be wrong about some of them.

Horizon 01 · 12 months

Reliable agents at scale.

8-hour run reliability ≥ 90%

A single Prometheus run that goes the full workday without intervention, on real customer workloads, on a clear majority of attempts.

Live retrieval at any context length

No retrieval cliff at 200 K, 500 K, or 1 M tokens — the same latency and answer quality at every length we serve.

Cross-lingual reasoning parity

Closing the English / non-English gap on reasoning evals to within 3 points across our 28 languages.

Horizon 02 · 24 months

Models that ship their own work.

Self-supervised process reward

Agents that grade their own intermediate steps and improve from outcomes, with verifiable feedback on every claim before it is acted on.

Customer-controlled fine-tuning

Bring-your-own-data fine-tuning where the data never leaves the customer's tenant, with quality competitive with central training.

Audit-grade tool use

Every tool call provenance-stamped, every action reversible, every chain of custody admissible to compliance.

Horizon 03 · long-term bets

Intelligence we can defend in writing.

Models with calibrated taste

Systems that prefer the better of two answers for the right reasons, and can explain the preference in language a human can verify.

Compute-aware autonomy

Models that decide when to spend more compute, when to ask for help, and when to stop — without a human writing the rule.

A platform you can audit end to end

From training-data provenance through inference-time decisions to the action that landed in your inbox. Auditable from the outside, not from a press release.

These horizons are revised every six months. The open questions below are where we currently see the largest gap between what we want and what works.

Open questions

What we are still working on.

The work that's still hard. Each of these is a problem we're spending real research budget on; the ones we figure out first will land in product first.

01

Long-horizon autonomy

Agents that hold their head over a full workday without human nudging — and recover gracefully when the world changes mid-run.

02

Useful-work evaluations

Benchmarks that capture the messy, multi-tool work people actually do, not just the academic puzzles a model can be fine-tuned to crush.

03

Honest refusals

Saying no for the right reasons, not the easy ones. Refusal calibration without over-refusal — a single number we still don't trust ourselves on.

04

Permission-aware retrieval

Indexing that respects access controls at every layer and never leaks a document to a teammate who couldn't already see it.

05

Auditable reasoning

Reasoning chains we can trust by reading them, not by hoping. Process supervision that survives contact with adversarial prompts.

06

Multimodal grounding

Cutting hallucination on charts, slides, and diagrams — the long tail of "the model said X but the chart says Y" failures.

07

Replayable agent runtimes

Determinism across retries, branching sub-agents, and partial tool failures, so "what happened in that 4-hour run?" is a one-click question.

08

Cross-lingual parity

Closing the quality gap between English and the next 27 languages on reasoning tasks, not just on translation and chat.

09

Compute-aware routing

Routing decisions that take cost, latency, and quality targets together — and learn from the outcomes of every prior turn.

10

Privacy-preserving fine-tuning

Letting customers shape model behaviour on their data without anything leaving their tenant — and without sacrificing quality.

We publish progress on these in our research log, and fund external work on a few of them through grants. If you have a serious answer to one of these, we want to read it.
